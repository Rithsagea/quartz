## Problem M.7
>[!question]
>Let $R=\mathbb Z[x]$.
>
>(a) Prove that every maximal ideal of $R$ has the form $(f,p)$, where $p$ is an integer prime and $f$ is a primitive integer polynomial that is irreducible modulo $p$
>
>(b) Let $I$ be an ideal of $R$ generated by two polynomials $f$ and $g$ that have no common factors other than $\pm1$. Prove that $R/I$ is finite.

### Part A

Let $I$ be a maximal ideal of $R$. Suppose $I$ does not contain an integer. Lifting this ideal to $\mathbb Q$, it will become principal with generator, say $f$. Let $f=c_0f'$, where $f'$ is primitive, and $p$ be a prime that does not divide any coefficients of $f'$. Then $R>(I,p)>I$, and $I$ is not maximal. Thus by contradiction, $I$ must contain an integer.

Let $a=\min(I\cap \mathbb Z)$. If $a$ is not prime, then we can write $a=pq$ for integers $p,q\not\in I$. Then $(p+I)(q+I)=0+I$, suggesting the existence of a zero divisor and contradiction the property of integral domain. Thus $a$ must be prime.

Let $p$ be a prime, and consider the ring homomorphism $R\to\mathbb F_p[x]$ obtained by modding out $(p)$. $\mathbb F_p[x]$ is a PID, so all maximal ideals are of the form $(f)$, where $f$ is an irreducible polynomial. There is a bijective correspondence between maximal ideals of $R$ containing $(p)$ and the maximal ideals of $\mathbb F_p[x]$. Therefore the maximal ideals of $R$ containing a prime $p$ are precisely the of the form $(f,p)$ where $f$ is irreducible modulo $p$.

### Part B

The unique factorization of $f$ and $g$ must not have any shared terms. Lifting these polynomials to $\mathbb Q[x]$, we have $\gcd(f,g)=1$. With Bezout's Identity, there exist $r,s\in\mathbb Q[x]$ such that $rf+sg=1$. Then there exists primitives $r',s'$ such that $r'f+s'g=x$ for some constant $a\in I$

<u>the rest of the proof is left as an exercise</u>

## Student Question
>[!question]
>Show that $i\not\in\mathbb Q[\sqrt{-2}]$.

We have previously shown that $i\not\in\mathbb Q[\sqrt2]$, so $\mathbb Q[\sqrt2,i]$ has degree 4.

Note that $\sqrt{-2}=i\sqrt2$, so $\mathbb Q[\sqrt2,i]=\mathbb Q[\sqrt{-2},i]$. Suppose $i\in\mathbb Q[\sqrt{-2}]$, implying $\mathbb Q[\sqrt{-2},i]=\mathbb Q[\sqrt{-2}]$. This gives a contradiction, as these two field extensions have different degrees.
