## Terminology
| Property                   | Statement                                    | Ideals                               |
| -------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| $u$ is a unit              | $u$ has mult. inverse in $R$                 | $(u)=1$                              |
| $a$ divides $b$            | $b=aq$ for $q\in R$                          | $(b)\subset(a)$                      |
| $a$ proper divisor of $b$  | $b=aq$ $a,q$ where not unit                  | $(b)<(a)<(1)$                        |
| $a$ and $b$ are associates | $b=ua$ where $u$ unit                        | $(a)=(b)$                            |
| $a$ is irreducible         | $a$ nonunit, no prop. div.                   | $(a)<\nexists(c)<(1)$                |
| $p$ is prime               | $p\vert ab$ implies $p\vert a$ or $p\vert b$ | if $ab\in(p)$ $a\text{ or }b\in (p)$ |

### Euclidean Domains
Examples: $\mathbb Z$, $\mathbb F[x]$, $\mathbb Z[i]$

For a domain $R$, the existence of a <u>size function</u> $\sigma:R-\{0\}\to\mathbb Z_{>0}$ implies $R$ is a <u>Euclidean domain</u>, if $\forall a,b\in R$, $a\ne 0$, $\exists q,r\in R$ such that $b=aq+r$ where $r=0$ or $\sigma(r)<\sigma(a)$

### Principal Ideal Domain
Examples: $\mathbb Z[\frac{-1+\sqrt{-19}}{2}]$

An integral domain where every ideal is principal is a <u>principal ideal domain</u>.

Let $a,b\in R$ an integral domain not both zero. A <u>greatest common divisor</u> $d$ of $a$ and $b$ is an element where $d|a$ and $d|b$. If $e|a$, $e|b$, then $e|d$.

If the $\gcd$ is a unit, then $a$ and $b$ are <u>relatively prime</u>. This will always exist in a PID. 

In a PID, an element is irreducible IFF it is a prime element. Furthermore, the maximal ideals are principal ideals generated by irreducible elements.

### Unique Factorization Domain
Examples: $\mathbb Z[x]$, $\mathbb Z[x_1,\ldots,x_n]$

A domain $R$ is a <u>unique factorization domain</u> if for all elements, factorization terminates and is unique up to associates.

This is equivalent to stating that $R$ does not have an infinite strictly increasing chain of principal ideals.

In an integral domain, if every irreducible element is a prime element, then the domain is a UFD

Any pair of elements has a $\gcd$.

### Integral Domain
Examples: $\mathbb Z[\sqrt{-5}]$

An <u>integral domain</u> or domain is a ring where there are no zero divisors, that is, $ab=0$ implies $a=0$ or $b=0$.

In an integral domain, prime elements are irreducible.

## Gauss Lemma
For a prime integer $p$, denote $\psi_p:\mathbb Z[x]\to\mathbb F_p[x]$. $f\in\ker\psi_p$ iff $p$ divides the coefficients of $f$, i.e. $p|f$ in $\mathbb Z[x]$.

$f(x)=a_nx^n+\cdots+a_1x+a_0$ is <u>primitive</u> when $\gcd(a_0,\ldots,a_n)$ is 1 and $a_n>0$

Integers are prime elements in $\mathbb Z[x]$, so $p|fg$ iff $p|f$ or $p|g$. Moreover (Gauss Lemma) the product of primitive polynomials is primitive.

## Factoring Integer Polynomials
Let $f$ be an integer polynomial, and $p$ a prime that does not divide leading coefficient of $f$. If $f$ is irreducible in $\mathbb F_p[x]$, then $f$ is irreducible in $\mathbb Q[x]$.

<u>Eisenstein Criterion</u> Let $f(x)=a_nx^n+\ldots+a_1x+a_0$ be an integer polynomial and $p$ a prime integer. If $p$ does not divide $a_n$, p divides $a_{n-1}\ldots a_0$, and $p^2$ does not divide $a_0$, then $f$ is irreducible in $\mathbb Q[x]$
