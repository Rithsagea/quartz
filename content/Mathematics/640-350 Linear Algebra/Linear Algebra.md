## Basic Spaces

### Fields
A field $F$ has two operations, $\cdot$ and $+$. They must satisfy the following properties
1. **Commutativity** $a+b=b+a$, $a\cdot b=b\cdot a$
2. **Associativity** $(a+b)+c=a+(b+c)$, $(a\cdot b)\cdot c=a\cdot(b\cdot c)$
3. **Identity** $0+a=a$, $1\cdot a=a$
4. **Inverses** $a+(-a)=0$, $b\cdot b^{-1}=1$
5. **Distributive** $a\cdot(b+c)=a\cdot b+a\cdot c$

### Vector Spaces
A vector space $V$ over a field $F$ must satisfy the following properties
1. **Commutativity of Addition** $x+y=y+x$
2. **Associativity of Addition** $(x+y)+z=x+(y+z)$
3. **Additive Identity** $x+0=x$
4. **Additive Inverse** $x+(-x)=0$
5. **Multiplicative Identity** $1x=x$
6. **Associativity of Multiplication** $(ab)x=a(bx)$
7. **Distributivity of Scalars** $a(x+y)=ax+ay$
8. **Distributivity of Vectors** $(a+b)x=ax+bx$

### Subspaces
A subspace $W\subseteq V$ must satisfy the following properties
1. **Additive Closure** $\forall x,y\in W:x+y\in W$
2. **Multiplicative Closure** $c\in F,x\in W:cx\in W$
3. **Zero** $0\in W$
4. **Inverse** $\forall x\in W:-x\in W$

### Linear Dependence
For a vector space $S$ over field $F$, a **linear combination** of $u_1,\ldots,u_n\in S$ and scalars $a_1,\ldots,a_n\in F$ is 
$$v=a_1u_1+\ldots+a_nu_n$$

The **span** of $S\subseteq V$ is the set of all linear combinations of vectors in $S$, denoted $\text{span}(S)$. By definition, $\text{span}(\varnothing)=\{0\}$.

A subset $S$ **generates** $V$ if $\text{span}(S)=V$

A subset $S\subseteq V$ is **linearly dependent** if there are vectors $u_1,\ldots,u_n\in S$ and scalars not all zero $a_1,\ldots,a_n\in F$ such that
$$a_1u_1+\ldots+a_nu_n=0$$

### Bases and Dimension
A **basis** $\beta$ for $V$ is a linearly independent subset of $V$ that generates $V$.

> [!info] Replacement Theorem
> For $V$ generated by $G$ containing $n$ vectors, take a linearly independent $L\subset V$ with $m$ vectors. Then $m\le n$ and there is $H\subset G$ with $n-m$ vectors such that $L\cup H$ generates $V$

The **dimension** of $V$ is the size of any basis, denoted $\dim(V)$. A vector space is **finite-dimensional** if there is a basis with a finite number of vectors.

## Linear Transformations

A **linear transformation** from $V$ to $W$ is denoted $T:V\rightarrow W$ if the following are satisfied
1. $T(x+y)=T(x)+T(y)$
2. $T(cx)=cT(x)$

Some addition properties of $T$ are
1. $T(0)=0$
2. $T(cx+y)=cT(x)+T(y)$
3. $T(x-y)=T(x)-T(y)$
4. $T\left(\sum^n_{i=1}a_ix_i\right)=\sum^n_{i=1}a_iT(x_i)$

The **identity transformation** over $V$ is $I_V(x)=x$, and the **zero transformation** is $T_0(x)=0$.

For a given linear transformation $T:V\rightarrow W$, the **null space** $N(T)=\{x\in V:T(x)=0\}$ is the set of all vectors $x\in V$ such that $T(x)=0$, and the **range** $R(T)=\{T(x):x\in V\}$ is the set of all possible output vectors.

From linearity, the null space and range are subspaces.

> [!info] Dimension Theorem
> For linear $T:V\rightarrow W$, then $\text{nullity}(T)+\text{rank}(T)=\dim(V)$

For linear transformations $U$ and $T$, the composition can be denoted $UT$ or $U\circ T$.

For linear $U$ and $T$, both $UT$ and $aT+U$ are linear.

$\mathcal L(V,W)$ is the set of all linear transformations from $V$ to $W$

### Matrix Representations

An **ordered basis** of $V$ is a basis with a specific order, or a finite sequence of linearly independent vectors that generates $V$.

For basis $\beta=\{u_1,\ldots,u_n\}$ and $x\in V$ with representation $x=\sum^n_{i=1}a_iu_i$, then the **coordinate vector** $x$ relative to $\beta$ is
$$[x]_\beta=\pmatrix{a_1\\\vdots\\a_n}$$

For a linear transformation $T:V\rightarrow W$ with basis $\beta$ of $V$ and $\gamma$ of $W$, the **matrix representation** is $[T]_\beta^\gamma$, or $[T]_\beta$ if $\beta=\gamma$.

$M_{n\times m}(F)$ is the set of all $n\times m$ matrices over the field $F$.

Note that $[T+U]^\gamma_\beta=[T]^\gamma_\beta+[U]^\gamma_\beta$, $[aT]^\gamma_\beta=a[T]^\gamma_\beta$, and $[UT]^\gamma_\alpha=[U]^\gamma_\beta[T]^\beta_\alpha$

For a matrix $A$, $L_A:v\rightarrow Av$ is the **left multiplication transform**. In this sense, matrix multiplication is analogous to composition of transformations.

### Invertibility

For linear $T:V\rightarrow W$, $U:W\rightarrow V$ is the **inverse** if $TU=I_W$. If $T$ has an inverse, it is **invertible**. For invertible functions $T$ and $U$, the following properties hold
1. $(TU)^{-1}=U^{-1}T^{-1}$
2. $(T^{-1})^{-1}=T$

For vector spaces $V$ and $W$, if there is an invertible $T:V\rightarrow W$, $V$ and $W$ are **isomorphic** and $T$ is an **isomorphism**.

For vector spaces $V$ and $W$ over $F$ with dimension $n$ and $m$ respectively, take $\beta$ and $\gamma$ to be their ordered bases. Then $\Phi^\gamma_\beta:\mathcal L(V,W)\rightarrow M_{m\times n}(F)$ given by $\Phi^\gamma_\beta(T)=[T]^\gamma_\beta$ is an isomorphism.

## Systems 
### Elementary Matrix Operations

For an $m\times n$ matrix $A$, the following are **elementary row operations**. (these also exist for columns)
1. interchanging two rows
2. multiplying any row with a nonzero scalar
3. adding a scalar multiple of one row to another

Each elementary operation has a corresponding **elementary matrix** $E$, where the representative row or column operation is equivalent to left or right multiplication.

Elementary matrices are invertible, and any invertible matrix can be represented as a composition of finite elementary row matrices.

### Systems
A system of equations can be represented as a matrix equation $Ax=b$. This system is **homogeneous** if $b=0$, and **nonhomogeneous** if not.

Let $K$ be the solution set of $Ax=b$ and $K_H$ the solution set of the homogeneous system $Ax=0$, Then for any solution $s$ to $Ax=b$.
$$K=\{s\}+K_H=\{s+k:k\in K_H\}$$

## Determinants

$\tilde A_{i,j}$ is the matrix obtained by removing the row $i$ and column $j$ from the matrix $A$.

The **determinant** for $A\in M_{n\times n}(F)$ is given as follows. If $n=1$, then $\det(A)=A_{1,1}$. For $n\ge 2$
$$\det(A)=\sum^n_{j=1}(-1)^{1+j}A_{1,j}\cdot\det(\tilde A_{1,j})$$

The scalar $(-1)^{i+j}\det(\tilde A_{i,j})$ is the **cofactor** of $A$ in row $i$, column $j$

$$\det\pmatrix{a_1\\\vdots\\u+kv\\\vdots\\a_n}
=\det\pmatrix{a_1\\\vdots\\u\\\vdots\\a_n}
+k\det\pmatrix{a_1\\\vdots\\v\\\vdots\\a_n}
$$

When applying elementary row operations to $A$, the determinant will change in only so many ways
1. If $B$ is obtained by interchanging rows of $A$, then $\det(B)=-\det(A)$
2. If $B$ is obtained by scaling a row of $A$ by k, then $\det(B)=k\det(A)$
3. If $B$ is obtained by adding a multiple of one row to another in $A$, then $\det(B)=\det(A)$

For $A,B\in M_{n\times n}(F)$, $\det(AB)=\det(A)\cdot\det(B)$. If $A$ is invertible, then $\det (A^{-1})=\det(A)^{-1}$

> [!info] Cramer's Rule
> For the system $Ax=b$ of $n$ linear equations and $n$ unknowns, if $\det(A)\ne 0$, then the system has a unique solution, where for each $k=1,\ldots,n$
> $$x_k=\frac{\det(M_k)}{\det(A)}$$
> Where $M$ is the $N\times n$ matrix obtained by replacing column $k$ of $A$ by $b$

## Diagonalization
### Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors
A linear operator $T:V\rightarrow V$ is **diagonalizable** if there is some basis $\beta$ such that $[T]_\beta$ is a diagonal matrix.

A vector $v\in V$ is an **eigenvector** of $T$ if there is a scalar **eigenvalue** $\lambda$ such that $T(v)=\lambda v$. The set of all eigenvectors for an eigenvalue $\lambda$ is the eigenspace, $E_\lambda$.

If a matrix is diagonalizable, then taking a basis of eigenvectors $\{v_1,\ldots,v_n\}$ and then constructing the matrix $Q$, $Q^{-1}AQ$ will be a diagonal matrix, made up of the eigenvalues of $A$.

$v$ is also an eigenvector of $A$, if for the eigenvalue $\lambda$ $(A-\lambda I_n)v=0$

The **characteristic polynomial** of $A$ is given $f(t)=\det(A-tI_n)$. This polynomial **splits** if there are scalars such that $f(t)=c(t-a_1)(t-a_2)\ldots(t-a_n)$. The characteristic polynomial splits if the corresponding linear map is diagonalizable.

The **algebraic multiplicity** of $\lambda$ is the largest $k$ such that $(t-\lambda)^k$ divides $f(t)$. The **geometric multiplicity** is the dimension of the corresponding eigenspace $E_\lambda$.

### Invariant Subspaces
For a linear operator $T$ on $V$, $W\subseteq V$ is a $T$-**invariant subspace** if $T(W)\subseteq W$. For some $x\in V$, the $T$-**cyclic subspace** of $V$ generated by $x$ is $\text{span}(\{x,T(x),T^2(x),\ldots\})$.

The characteristic polynomial of $T_W$ (restriction of $T$ on $W$) divides the characteristic polynomial of $T$.

The **Cayley-Hamilton** theorem states that applying the characteristic polynomial to its linear operator will return the zero transformation $f(T)=T_0$, and similar results will hold for the respective matrix $f(A)=\mathbb 0$.

## Jordan Canonical Form
A **Jordan block** is a matrix with a constant $\lambda$ on the diagonal, and 1s on the upper super-diagonal.
$$\pmatrix{\lambda&1&0&0\\0&\lambda&1&0\\0&0&\lambda&1\\0&0&0&\lambda}$$

A **Jordan canonical form** is a matrix where the diagonals are made up of Jordan blocks, and everything else is 0. A **Jordan canonical basis** $\beta$ for $T$ is one such that $[T]_\beta$ is a Jordan canonical form.

A **generalized eigenvector** of $T$ corresponding to $\lambda$ satisfies $(T-\lambda I)^p(x)=0$ for some positive integer $p$. The **generalized eigenspace** is denoted $K_\lambda$.

For some generalized eigenvector $x$, then the **cycle of generalized eigenvectors** is $\{(T-\lambda I)^{p-1}(x),\ldots,(T-\lambda I(x),x\}$, where $p$ is the minimal p such that $(T-\lambda I)^p(x)=0$. The **initial vector** is $(T-\lambda I)^{p-1})(x)$, and the **end vector** is $x$. This is a cycle of length $p$.

Two matrices $A$ and $B$ are **similar** if they can be be brought to the same Jordan canonical form with a choice of basis.
